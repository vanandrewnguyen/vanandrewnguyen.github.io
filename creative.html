Pixel Art



Pixel art's a pretty ugly medium by itself so it makes sense to use techniques which hide its ugly aesthetic. One way to do this is to anti alias (place pixels of a similiar colour, think blending but to show detail rather than transitions) heavily.
However, if you embrace its limitations by going the opposite route, you end up with some very stark results.
Exhibit below, playing with monochromatic schemes is pretty fun.

Spooktober 2018 Art Challenge
See the use of monochrome with stray pixels as a dither texture.

Spooktober 2019 Art Challenge
See the use of dither, this time blended with multiple shades.

Playing with volumes that have character. I probably could go back and dither the clouds to have it look softer.

Basic volumes and character pose. Posing is the biggest issue, because the canvas is so limited it's often hard to distinguish between set poses with pixel-art.

This was a postcard I made for a friend, it's meant to be a scene depicting Sydney trains on a sunny evening.

Made an album cover for my game project. Had to anti-alias for minor features, e.g. wrinkles and edge illumination.


Game screenshots



Post-processing makes pixel art much easier to consume. You can get away with crude, 'crunchy' pixel-art (see below) when you add gradient noise and other screen-space effects to vary the colour output. Personally I hate the classic toon cel-shading, most modern games blend it with traditional diffuse and bloom (like below).

Horror-esque game, note the procedural background blending. The meta-balls are dynamic, whereas the textures are pre-baked and tiled.

Shader Screenshots



These shaders below generated with one script each!. Shader writing is generally more technical than artistic, but man, playing around with the positioning and colouring models makes you *feel* like an artiste.

This one's pretty cool because you can fake atmospheric effects as a 2D texture instead of a 3D algo. Included this because an optimisation decision is still an artistic one :)

I love that scene in Disney's Up when Carl navigates through the thunderstorm, been trying to recreate that effect ever since I started graphics programming. This is as far as I've gotten.

Lighting a scene is probably the most aesthetically important decision you can make, this is a crude, boring raymarch of a mountainside, but the lighting just elevates it to look better than it does.

You can get creative with the noise algos you put in, in this case you can generate canyon-like features by blocking off sections. It's very much an artistic choice, and what's even better is that I'm lazy and I used the exact same noise algos to shade these canyons as well!!! Work smarter not harder :)
